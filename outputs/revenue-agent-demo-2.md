## 1. Market Signal Brief

### Demand Pattern
Real, growing local-service category, not a niche curiosity: Mobile Pet Grooming market $1.7B (2025) -> $3.1B (2034), ~9.0% CAGR. Mobile/at-home grooming grew 37% over three years. Driver: pet humanization + convenience demand. Per-van economics: $75-120/groom, 8-15 appointments/day, $75K-200K annual revenue/van, 50-70% EBITDA margins. Startup cost $25K-180K. 58% of bookings now happen online.

Inference (unverified): "booked out / long waitlist" is a common trade-press narrative but not confirmed by direct customer complaint data — treat as plausible, not citable.

### Audience Psychology
Two audiences: (1) prospective mobile-groomer operators — want proof this is a real income vehicle, fear van/equipment capex and customer acquisition, want a faster path to full bookings than word-of-mouth. Beginner-to-intermediate. (2) FuturIQ's real viewer — wants proof the AI agent pipeline generalizes to local/appointment-based service businesses (the model most of THEIR audience runs), and will write off the demo as templated if the offer/funnel looks identical to the teddy-bear e-commerce run.

### Opportunity Score: 26/35
- Urgency: 3/5
- Willingness to pay: 4/5
- Searchability: 4/5
- Clickability: 4/5
- Credibility: 3/5 (weakest link — no case study exists yet)
- Business fit: 4/5
- Under-15-min viability: 4/5

### Risk
Generic if not explicitly contrasted against the teddy-bear e-commerce run. Unbelievable without a specific derived mechanism (not just restating industry stats). Risk of audience confusion if not quickly reframed as "this is about local service businesses in general."

### Strategic Recommendation
- Frame the offer around calendar-fill / recurring-booking density (8-15 stops/day economics), not generic "grooming marketing."
- Explicitly position as the local/appointment-based counterpart to the e-commerce demo.
- Build the mechanism as a buildable template, not a claimed proven result (no case study yet).
- Target service-area targeting and route-density optimization — unique to local service.

## 2. Offer Architecture

### Positioning
Same agent team, second business idea — the system doesn't reuse the teddy-bear funnel. It detects "this is calendar capacity, not cart conversion" and rebuilds the mechanism from scratch, live. The core sellable offer is still the Revenue Agent Team template, never the pet-grooming business itself.

### Core Offer
Still the **Revenue Agent Team** template/workshop/done-with-you build. What changes is the proof: the system now has demonstrated range across two business model types, letting FuturIQ sell it as a system rather than a script that only works on one kind of business.

### Unique Mechanism: "Business-Model-Aware Agent Routing"
The agent team classifies the business as transactional/e-commerce vs. recurring/appointment-based, then branches the mechanism accordingly:
- Teddy bears -> AOV / scarcity / cart-conversion.
- Grooming van -> calendar-capacity / route-density / rebooking-cadence.

This branching logic, shown explicitly on screen, is the credibility engine — an e-commerce-only or prompt-engineering demo could never surface it.

### Value Ladder

## 4. Conversion System

### Lead Magnet: "The Local Service Revenue Agent Pack — Starter Kit Vol. 2"
**Promise:** The three appointment-based agent prompts built live in this video, copy-paste ready, plus a one-page Business Model Router showing which of five revenue classifications a business falls into.

**Contents:**
1. Three working Claude Code agent prompts, verbatim: service-area scorer, route-density calculator, rebooking-cadence generator.
2. The Business Model Router — a one-page decision tree exposing the classification logic (calendar-capacity / route-density-rebooking / AOV-scarcity / subscription-churn / project-retainer), self-serviceable without running the agents.
3. A "Vol. 1 vs Vol. 2" architecture map — what stayed the same (the agent team OS) vs. what changed (the routing output) between kits.

**Relationship to Video 1's kit:** Explicitly **Volume 2**, not a rehash — Vol. 1 = e-commerce/AOV-scarcity config, Vol. 2 = local-service/calendar-capacity config. The architecture map ships inside both kits so downloaders discover the other exists, teaching for free that the system is modular by design — which is exactly what the premium offer sells.

### In-Video CTA (12:00-13:00, word for word)
"If you've been watching this thinking 'okay, but what would this build for my business' — that's exactly why I put this together. It's called the Local Service Revenue Agent Pack, and it's not a rehash of what I gave away in the teddy bear video. It's volume two. You get the three agent prompts I just ran on screen — copy, paste, run them tonight. But the real piece is the Router. It's the one-page map of the exact five ways this pipeline classifies a business, so you can find yours in about two minutes instead of guessing. Link's in the description, it's free, go grab it before you try to build any of this yourself. Because here's the thing — the money was never in the tool. It's in the system that adapts."

### Follow-Up Sequence
1. **Day 0** — "Your Local Service Revenue Agent Pack (open this before you build anything)" — orient to the Router first, prompts second. CTA: run the Router, reply with your classification.
2. **Day 2** — "The part of this video I didn't hide" — own the credibility gap directly, reframe as an invitation to be an early data point. CTA: reply with your business type.
3. **Day 4** — "Why route-density beats AOV for a business like yours" — teach the routing mechanism deeper, plant that judgment calls need more than a free pack. CTA: tease the workshop.
4. **Day 6** — "Build Your Revenue Agent Team in a Weekend (cohort opens — limited seats)" — direct workshop pitch, structural urgency via real seat cap/close date. CTA: grab a seat.
5. **Day 9** — "Last call, and the other option if a weekend isn't it" — close the workshop window, introduce the done-with-you AI Systems Build for those who want it built for them. CTA: grab a seat or reply "build."

### Sales Path
The Router is the hinge: it turns a passive "interesting demo" reaction into an active personal question ("what does this say about MY business?") that no free asset can fully answer — answering it right requires judgment calls specific to the person's numbers. That's the honest, non-manufactured reason to want the workshop. The workshop forks two ways at close: people who build something they like become case-study material (solving the credibility gap from Email 2); people who want it built *for* them become premium done-with-you clients.

### Friction Check
- Two lead magnets in one funnel could confuse anyone who missed Video 1 — fixed by including the Vol. 1 vs Vol. 2 map inside the pack and offering Vol. 1 as a passive add-on, not a hard redirect.
- The credibility gap, if left unaddressed, becomes the biggest friction point — Email 2 exists specifically to name it before the reader does.
- Email 2's reply-ask needs to clearly give something back (a personal classification answer), not read as unpaid research.
- Cohort urgency in Email 4 must be real, or it breaks the "helpful not pushy" brand voice.
- The Router must only classify, never fully build the viewer's specific agent configuration — or there's no reason left to attend the workshop.
